# Heart Health Notes

This document summarizes key insights from trusted health resources about **Resting Heart Rate (RHR)** and **Heart Rate Variability (HRV)**.

---

# Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

**Source:** Mayo Clinic  
https://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-lifestyle/fitness/expert-answers/heart-rate/faq-20057979

## What is Resting Heart Rate?

Resting Heart Rate (RHR) is the number of times your heart beats per minute while your body is completely at rest.

## Normal Range

- Normal adult resting heart rate: **60–100 BPM**
- Well-trained athletes may have resting heart rates around **40 BPM**
- In general, **a lower resting heart rate indicates better cardiovascular fitness**

## How to Measure

Measure your pulse:

- At the wrist (radial artery)
- At the neck (carotid artery)

Best time:

- Immediately after waking up
- Before getting out of bed

Count beats for:

- 15 seconds × 4
- or 30 seconds × 2

## Factors That Affect Resting Heart Rate

- Age
- Fitness level
- Sleep quality
- Emotional stress
- Smoking
- Diabetes
- High cholesterol
- Heart disease
- Body position
- Certain medications

## Smartwatch Tracking

Wearables can estimate resting heart rate and are useful for tracking long-term trends, although readings may not always be perfectly accurate.

## When to Seek Medical Advice

Consult a healthcare provider if:

- Resting heart rate is consistently **above 100 BPM**
- Resting heart rate is consistently **below 60 BPM** (and you are not an athlete)
- You experience:
  - Dizziness
  - Fainting
  - Shortness of breath

## Key Takeaways

- Lower isn't always better—it should be normal for **you**.
- Monitor long-term trends instead of individual readings.
- Significant unexplained changes deserve medical evaluation.

# Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

**Source:** Oura Health  
https://ouraring.com/blog/average-hrv/

## What is HRV?

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) measures the variation (in milliseconds) between consecutive heartbeats.

It reflects how well your autonomic nervous system adapts to stress and recovery.

## What HRV Indicates

Higher HRV generally suggests:

- Better recovery
- Better cardiovascular fitness
- Greater resilience to stress
- Healthy parasympathetic ("rest-and-digest") activity

Lower HRV may indicate:

- Physical or mental stress
- Poor recovery
- Illness
- Fatigue
- Overtraining

## There Is No Perfect HRV

HRV varies greatly between individuals.

The most meaningful comparison is:

- ✅ Your HRV today vs. your own long-term baseline
- ❌ Your HRV vs. someone else's

## Average HRV (Oura Community Data)

- Overall average: **41 ms**
- Women:
  - Average: **41.5 ms**
  - Median: **35 ms**
- Men:
  - Average: **40.3 ms**
  - Median: **34.2 ms**
- Typical healthy values can range from **20–200 ms**, depending on the individual.

## HRV Changes With Age

- HRV naturally decreases as people age.
- Younger individuals generally have higher HRV.
- Healthy habits may slow age-related decline.

## Factors That Influence HRV

### Lifestyle Factors

- Sleep quality
- Stress management
- Exercise
- Nutrition
- Hydration
- Alcohol consumption
- Caffeine intake

### Non-Modifiable Factors

- Age
- Genetics
- Sex
- Hormones

## Improving HRV

Recommended habits:

- Maintain consistent, high-quality sleep
- Exercise regularly without overtraining
- Eat a balanced diet
- Stay hydrated
- Reduce chronic stress
- Practice meditation or breathing exercises
- Limit alcohol
- Be patient—improvements often take weeks

## Key Takeaways

- Focus on trends rather than single-night readings.
- Stable HRV over time is more valuable than achieving extremely high values.
- Temporary drops in HRV can result from:
  - Poor sleep
  - Illness
  - Alcohol
  - High stress
  - Intense exercise
- Returning to your baseline is generally a sign of healthy recovery.

# Quick Summary

| Metric | Healthy Indicator | Watch For |
|---------|-------------------|------------|
| Resting Heart Rate (RHR) | Lower within your normal range | Persistently >100 BPM or <60 BPM (non-athletes) |
| Heart Rate Variability (HRV) | Higher relative to your own baseline | Sustained decreases from your normal baseline |

## Core Principle

> **Don't compare yourself to others. Track your own long-term trends.**

Both Resting Heart Rate (RHR) and Heart Rate Variability (HRV) are most valuable when monitored consistently over time, rather than interpreting a single measurement in isolation.
